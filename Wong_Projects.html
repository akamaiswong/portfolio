Project: Scratch Project

Course: CSE (Computer Science and Engineering)

Duration: Approximately 2 weeks (due September 7th)

Objective: To build a project using Scratch which was user-friendly.

Type: Partner Project

For this project, I worked with a partner named Justin Tsai. We were tasked with coding either a game or story using Scratch, that implemented user interface and was relatively user-friendly with as little glitches as possible. We were given two introductions to Scratch, a basic one, and a more advanced one, then were allowed to make our own project. We decided to make a game where the main character was trying to break out of detention, and the player had to pick between two different places that the person could go, to help the person find his way out. The character told the player how to make him choose between different places, and at some places, the character ran into different people. This meant that we not only had to program the main sprite, but the other secondary ones as well. The game originally included making the player use arrow keys to control the sprite, but we changed the controls to use different lettered keys instead to make the game easier to play. We also made a project log to daily record the work that we did. In this project, Justin was mostly responsible for the flowchart and visuals of the project - he selected backgrounds, and images for the main sprites, while I was responsible for most of the coding He was the main visionary for this project, while I was the main programmer. I also completed the vast majority of the project log, (as linked above). Through this project, we gained a better understanding of the workings of Scratch, and I also learned how to work with a partner effectively.

Project: Python Image Manipulation Project

Course: CSE (Computer Science and Engineering)

Duration: Approximately 2 weeks (due March 25)

Objective: The objective was to create a brand image for a fictional or real company. It needed to represent their product in a memorable fashion. The purpose was to increase sales.

Type: Partner Project

For this project, I worked with a partner named Soumya Kakarlapudi. The instructions were to make a logo for a fiction or creative company, as explained in more detail in the objective above. The customer that we chose was Amazon, and we chose to work with their recent product, the Echo Dot, also known as Alexa. We compiled an amount of logos from the apps that Alexa can connect to, and resized and cropped them all to the same size. Afterwards, we pasted them, along with the actual Alexa logo onto an image of Alexa. We used a project log to document our progress in the project daily. A challenge we faced was in making the loops for the manipulation less file specific. We had to change our code from being file specific, to functions with loops to encompass all of the images. This was the part of the project that I focused on. Soumya focused mostly on coding the images, uploading them into the workspace and cropping them, while I did most of the coding for the loops and pasting. I also worked on the semi-transparency of the images. The images that we modified can be found in the project log (linked above) in original and modified forms - along with credits for the original images. This project was helpful in learning image manipulation in Python, and was also a great lesson in teamwork, leadership, and delegating different tasks to different people based on their strengths and weaknesses.

Project: Solar Hydrogen Vehicle

Course: POE (Principles of Engineering)

Duration: From Thursday, November 1st, 2018, to Friday, November 16 (Approximately 2 weeks)

Objective: The objective of this project was to find a configuration of solar or hydrogen cells in either a series or a parallel configuration to recommend to Tesla Motors. We would build a prototype vehicle to test out the configuration and show it to Tesla Motors as evidence of our findings.

Type: Team Project

For this project, I worked with partners Niharika Suravarjjala and Amelyn Phang. The instructions were to create a prototype of a vehicle that could be fueled by solar or hydrogen cells in different configurations, as explained in further detail in the objective above. The customer was Tesla Motors, as the project was inspired by Tesla Motor's recent environment friendly cars with renewable energy sources. We built the car with VEX parts, and a breadboard for wiring the energy sources. Throughout this project, we divided the project into several major parts, and each took main responsibility for one, but still helped with the others. For my part, I focused on documentation and wiring the breadboard with the different cell configurations. For the actual building of the car, I attached the standoffs and top layer of the car, to make our signature double-decker car look. Then, I contributed to the planning of the attachment of the cells to the top layer of the car. We used configurations of a single solar cell, two solar cells in parallel, a singular hydrogen cell, and two hydrogen cells in series. As discussed in our project log, we eventually found that the car was the most efficient with a singular solar cell wired to the motor. We thought this was because the car was built with a simple design meant to be as light as possible, and easy to put together for the manufacturers. This configuration was the most efficient, with an efficiency of 53%. Additionally, this design was the most practical. The single solar cell is easier to implement, as a singular solar cell is cheaper than any of the other options and is the easiest to install in a real life car.

Project: Aerodynamic Design

Course: POE (Principles of Engineering)

Duration: Tuesday, January 8th, 2019 - Wednesday, January 23rd, 2019 (Approximately 2.5 weeks)

Objective: Design, model, and test six projectile prototypes to see which model will travel the farthest distance, and which model is the overall best one to recommend to Nitro Planes.

Type: Team Project

For this project, I worked with partners Shriya Reddy and Jasleen Kaur. The instructions were to design and make two rocket projectiles each, to launch off of a launcher, as explained in further detail in the objective above. The customer was Nitro Planes, who wanted toy projectiles to sell alongside their new rocket launcher. We built these model projectiles out of paper products (printer paper, construction paper, etc.), and tape products (duct tape, masking tape, packing tape, etc.). This project was graded according to this rubric. Since we worked independently for the majority of the project time, we each produced our own results; I built two rockets like the other two team members, but I focused more on the content and formatting of the documentation than they did. After building our first rockets, we tested them in the Dublin High School football field. The experimental and theoretical results are shown in our documentation and simulation. We concluded that Design 6, designed and built by Jasleen Kaur was the best prototype because it went the furthest, 98 yards. This project was helpful in learning how to use Google Sheets more effectively, and about aerodynamics, because it focused on the flight of the projectiles, which was unlike any other project I have ever worked on before.
