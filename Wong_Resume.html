Field Trip: Simpson Strong-Tie (Pleasanton, California)

Course: POE (Principles of Engineering)

Date: May 1, 2019

I went on this field trip to Simpson Strong-Tie with the Dublin Engineering and Design Academy. During this field trip, we had the opportunity to take a tour of their facility and learn how the company does procedures such as stress tests on their products. I learned a lot about what goes into making a connector from beginning to end. The most interesting part about this field trip was learning about the variation in the products that are specifically made for different regions. For example, the parts that are built for buildings in California are made to be more resistant to earthquakes. This effect is produced by designing the connectors between tall building levels to allow the building to sway from side to side during an earthquake rather than just crack immediately. I was fascinated to learn how small changes like this could protect a building against a large natural disaster like an earthquake.

Guest Speakers

Guest Speaker: Maynard Holliday

Course: POE (Principles of Engineering)

Topic: Chernobyl, Robotics, and Artificial Intelligence

Date: October 15, 2018

One of the guest speakers I had the honor of listening to while taking the class POE (Principles of Engineering) was Maynard Holliday. He was previously the senior technical advisor to the United States of America's undersecretary of defense for acquisition technology and logistics. He worked on the remediation for the Chernobyl nuclear accident, using both his skills in robotics and artificial intelligence. I found Holliday's presentation extremely interesting since I am very interested in learning more about artificial intelligence and machine learning. One key takeaway that I got from this presentation was that machine learning utilizes all of the following: speech recognition, digital assistance, fingerprint identification, computational photography, facial recognition, and producing text. In summary, this presentation was helpful to me because it was beneficial to see an industry professional's projects and his process of completing them.

Guest Speaker: Laura Kegelmeyer

Course: POE (Principles of Engineering)

Topic: NIF (National Ignition Facility at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory) & Machine Learning

Date: January 11, 2019

Another guest speaker in POE (Principles of Engineering) that presented in class was Laura Kegelmeyer who worked at the NIF (National Ignition Facility) at Lawrence Livermore Nation Laboratory. This presentation inspired me because it showed that there are useful applications for engineering this concept could help humanity, which has driven me to continue studying engineering in general and especially machine learning. Overall, Kegelmeyer's presentation helped me see the amount of good that engineers can do for the world by making technology and equipment work together with people to better the lives of many.

Guest Speaker: Jason Yosinski

Course: AP CSA (Advanced Placement Computer Science Applications)

Topic: Artificial Intelligence & Neural Networks

A guest speaker that we had the honor of meeting while in AP CSA was Jason Yosinski. He obtained his PhD from Cornell University and is a founding member of Uber AI Labs. He talked about his work with neural networks, and the different projects that he has worked on that implemented neural networks or artificial intelligence. A key takeaway from his presentation was the way neural networks work, and how computer scientists like him work to refine them so that they make less mistakes. In my opinion, his most interesting project to date is a project where he used neural networks to identify images. He would input an image and the network would tell him what it thought the image was, and how certain it was that it was that answer. The network identified images by looking for patterns. For example, there was one pattern of pure black, and one of pure white. Then, it looked for edges. For example, black on the left of a straight edge, and white on the right, or other such patterns. Through a trial and error system with many images, the neural network eventually got to the best it would be. The engineers found out that this was the end of refinements they could make to the system because some outliers would throw the system off, but the majority of the images would be recognized correctly.

Mentorship Program

In my junior year of high school, I had the amazing opportunity to participate in the Mentorship Program run by the Dublin Engineering and Design Academy. It pairs students with mentors in STEM industries to introduce students to options after high school, including trade school, community college, or a traditional four-year university. It also prepares students for college and a career after college if this is the route they want to take. I met weekly with other students in the academy, developing my skills in networking, interview etiquette, and more. Then, once a month, I met with my mentor to put these skills to the test. My mentor was Connie Nguyen, a software development engineer at Workday. She was very supportive and helped me learn more about college and careers from the perspective of someone who went to a four-year university and is now working in the computer science industry.
